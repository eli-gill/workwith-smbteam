Intake Assessment

Julia Jackson personally handles every intake call at Jackson Abdalla Law Group — and with 20–30 MetLife estate planning leads arriving every single day, she is receiving more demand than any solo attorney can physically answer. Callbacks are taking 2–3 days, sometimes nearly a week, and 40 MetLife cases have stalled entirely, representing an estimated $34,000 in missed monthly revenue from intake failure alone. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Julia personally answers every intake call — there is no delegation, no backup, and no coverage when she is with a client, in court, or unavailable
  • Voicemail callbacks take 2–3 days; some prospective clients wait nearly a week before hearing from the firm — and in estate planning, the client who waits that long has already called someone else
  • No after-hours coverage of any kind — calls received in the evening or on weekends go unanswered until Julia checks voicemail, creating a gap that is losing cases every day
  • No structured intake process, no defined scripts, and no follow-up sequence — each call is handled differently, and there is no system to nurture leads who do not sign on the first contact
Blind Spot: The MetLife leads that go unanswered are not patient — they are calling the next attorney on the referral list. When a MetLife client reaches voicemail, the window to win that case closes quickly. The firm may not know how many cases it is losing each week because there is no tracking system in place to count the missed calls or the resulting stalled cases beyond the 40 already identified.
The Gap
  • An estimated $34,000 per month in identified missed revenue from stalled MetLife cases and unanswered calls — this is not potential future revenue, it is demand that is already at the door and being turned away
  • 40 stalled MetLife cases represent both lost revenue and a reputational risk with MetLife's internal review system — slow response times threaten the firm's standing with its primary and only consistent lead source
  • Julia's response delays put the MetLife relationship itself at risk — the firm's entire business model depends on a referral relationship that requires a standard of responsiveness the current intake process cannot meet
Every intake call that goes unanswered is a family Julia cannot help and a paycheck that never arrives. Fixing this is the fastest path to both more revenue and more time — because the leads are already here.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • No contact form confirmed above the fold — prospective clients who reach the website cannot immediately request a consultation without navigating away from the homepage to find a form
  • No live chat observed on the site — a prospective client visiting at 8pm after the kids are in bed has no option to initiate contact outside of calling a phone number that goes to voicemail
  • Every barrier to contact on the website compounds the intake gap that already exists by phone — visitors who cannot reach the firm easily leave and call a competitor who makes it easier

Team Assessment

Julia Jackson is the entire team at Jackson Abdalla Law Group — attorney, intake coordinator, case manager, and administrator all in one person. After paying $26,000 upfront to a hire who produced zero work, the fear of hiring again is real and understandable. But the current reality, where Julia handles every function of the firm alone while working 70–80 hours per week, makes meaningful growth impossible. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Julia is the only employee — every task in the firm, from answering calls to preparing documents to managing cases, requires her personal involvement
  • A previous hire cost $26,000 upfront with no productive output — there were no documented processes, no training system, and no KPIs for the hire to work within
  • No documented workflows, no job descriptions, and no performance standards — hiring someone today would produce the same result as before, because there is nothing to hand them
  • Julia cannot take a single day off without the firm stalling completely — every case, every call, and every deadline depends on one person who is already burning out at 70–80 hours per week
Blind Spot: The previous $26,000 hiring failure was not a people problem — it was a systems problem. Without a documented intake process, a defined role, and measurable KPIs, no hire can succeed regardless of their ability. The next hire will produce the same result if the system is not built first. Hiring is not the solution; building the system that makes hiring work is the solution.
The Gap
  • Julia's time is physically capped at 70–80 hours per week — revenue cannot grow beyond what she can personally produce in those hours, and she is already at the ceiling
  • Without a team, Julia cannot take a vacation, cannot get sick, and cannot step back from any part of the business — she is the single point of failure for a firm that her family depends on
  • Every call Julia answers for intake is a call she is not spending on client work, case preparation, or the higher-value activities that actually grow the firm — the firm's best use of her time is being consumed by the firm's most replaceable function
Building a self-managing team is not just a business decision for Julia — it is the only path to getting her evenings back, being present for her family, and building something that does not require her to be everywhere at once. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Profit Plan Assessment

Jackson Abdalla Law Group generates approximately $200,000 per year in revenue — but Julia has zero financial visibility into what the firm actually keeps after expenses, what recurring costs are accumulating without her knowledge, or whether growth is building real wealth or just more complexity. Personal and business finances run through the same debit card, and significant recurring charges are being paid without awareness. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Personal and business expenses run through a single Bank of America debit card — accurate bookkeeping is impossible and true profit cannot be calculated
  • A $1,798 per month Levitate charge was discovered during this consultation — a significant recurring cost Julia was not aware she was paying, totaling over $21,000 per year
  • Duplicate Adobe subscriptions identified — an additional unnecessary recurring expense being paid without review
  • No bookkeeper engaged, no accounting system in place, and no monthly financial reports — Julia cannot determine what the firm earns after expenses or what she actually takes home
Blind Spot: Without separating personal and business finances, Julia cannot accurately calculate whether the firm is profitable — which means every decision about hiring, marketing investment, and pricing is being made without real data. The firm may be generating $200,000 in revenue but a significantly lower actual take-home profit, and there is currently no way to know the difference. Growth without this clarity could mean working harder for the same or less real income.
The Gap
  • Without financial visibility, Julia cannot make confident decisions about when to hire, how much to invest in marketing, or whether the firm is actually profitable after all expenses are accounted for
  • The identified unreviewed recurring charges alone — Levitate at $1,798/month plus duplicate subscriptions — represent thousands of dollars per year in avoidable costs that could be redirected toward growth
  • Julia is the sole income earner for her family, working 70–80 hours per week, but has no accurate picture of what that work is actually producing in take-home income — financial clarity is not a luxury at this stage, it is a requirement
Julia cannot feel financially secure, and cannot build the future she is working toward, until she knows exactly what the firm earns, what it costs, and what she takes home. That clarity is not complicated to create — but it requires a system that does not currently exist.
