Intake Assessment

McLellan Law Group, LLP's primary intake system is Law Broker — and the firm is converting only 8–10% of incoming consultation requests into signed clients, meaning nearly 90% of prospects who raise their hand never become clients. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating. This is where the biggest immediate ROI lives.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • The Law Broker funnel converts at only 8–10% — the bottleneck is not a shortage of prospects but the system's inability to turn interested people into signed clients
  • The website CTA sends visitors off-site to Law Broker's form rather than capturing them on an embedded, on-site form — every visitor who leaves the site is a prospect the firm cannot follow up with or retarget
  • No after-hours intake coverage — prospects who visit the site in evenings or on weekends have no way to reach anyone immediately, making it likely they contact a competitor who responds faster
  • No defined follow-up sequence for prospects who complete the Law Broker form but do not immediately sign — at 8–10% conversion, the remaining 90% receive no systematic follow-up and are effectively abandoned
Blind Spot: The Law Broker form's 8–10% conversion rate may reflect what happens after the form is submitted more than it reflects lead quality. If prospects wait hours or days for a response, most will have already retained a competitor who replied within minutes. Speed-to-response is the single most predictive variable in legal intake conversion.
The Positive
  • The firm uses Clio to track billing data — existing practice management infrastructure that a structured intake workflow can plug directly into
  • Law Broker provides a documented funnel with a measurable conversion rate — the 8–10% baseline is a known number that a systematic process can target and improve
The Gap
  • At 8–10% conversion, improving to 25–30% would effectively triple signed clients from the same lead volume — this is the fastest possible revenue increase without spending more on marketing
  • Every inquiry that goes unanswered after business hours is a potential client permanently lost — in a market where competitors respond within minutes, next-day follow-up is not competitive
  • Without a systematic follow-up sequence for unconverted prospects, the firm is leaving signed cases on the table every month from leads it has already paid for through Law Broker
An intake system that converts 25% instead of 8% means the firm signs three times as many clients from the same leads — without either partner personally managing a single additional call.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • The homepage CTA ("Request a Free Consultation") links to lawbrokr.com rather than an embedded contact form on mclellanlawgroup.com — visitors who click the CTA leave the firm's domain, breaking the retargeting connection and the firm's ability to capture prospect data independently
  • No live chat is present on the website — prospects who visit outside business hours have no immediate way to connect, and after-hours inquiries are lost to competitors who offer instant response
  • A contact page exists at /contact-us but it is not featured above the fold — the primary conversion path routes visitors off-site, and the on-site alternative is buried

These findings are also referenced in the Website Audit section.

Team Assessment

McLellan Law Group, LLP has three attorneys and at least one operations team member — but both founding partners are deeply embedded in day-to-day work, with one partner identified as unable to delegate and the other describing the firm as stuck in the "Abyss" of a growth plateau where more revenue means more partner workload, not more freedom. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Steven struggles to delegate — operational decisions and casework flow through him personally, which means his capacity directly limits the firm's revenue ceiling
  • Claire has described the firm as being in the "Abyss" — a growth plateau where revenue is climbing but the operational systems have not kept pace, so every dollar of additional revenue requires proportionally more partner time to deliver
  • The firm has a third attorney (Elizabeth Handtke) but no dedicated operations manager, intake coordinator, or team lead to absorb delegated functions from the founding partners
  • The current growth strategy calls for conservative hiring (one additional attorney in 2027) — but without delegation systems and clear accountability structures in place first, adding headcount will increase coordination complexity before it reduces partner burden
Blind Spot: The greatest risk of the Abyss is not being stuck in it — it is staying there. Firms in the Abyss often hire to solve the bottleneck, but without delegation systems, new team members create more coordination work rather than less. The sequence matters: systems and accountability structures first, then headcount growth.
The Positive
  • Claire delegates effectively — this is a meaningful strength and a model the firm can build on; one partner is already operating the way the whole firm eventually needs to operate
  • Three attorneys with complementary strengths provide a foundation for specialization and delegation once the workflow and accountability systems are in place
The Gap
  • Steven's inability to delegate sets a hard ceiling on the firm's billable output — the firm cannot grow beyond what he can personally produce until case workflows are documented and transfer-ready
  • Without a self-managing layer between the partners and the work, Claire cannot step back even partially — every operational decision still requires a founding partner to resolve it
  • The long-term vision of a general counsel subscription model requires operational infrastructure — defined roles, accountability systems, and repeatable processes — that does not yet exist at the firm
A firm where Claire and Steven can step out for a week — or a month — and revenue continues and clients are served is not a fantasy. It is a system. Building that system is the work.

Profit Plan Assessment

McLellan Law Group, LLP billed $366,000 in 2025 but collected only approximately $320,000 — a 75–80% collection rate that represents roughly $46,000 in earned revenue that never reached the bank, against a $30,000 monthly break-even. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • A 75–80% collection rate means roughly $0.20–$0.25 of every billed dollar is not being collected — at $366k in billings, that is $46,000–$92,000 per year in uncollected fees the firm has already earned
  • The firm has no formal accounts receivable process — collections is a stated major pain point but has not been systematized, so recovering unpaid invoices depends on partner bandwidth and attention
  • The $30,000/month break-even creates real pressure in any month where collections fall below threshold — with a 75–80% collection rate and $366k in annual billings, the monthly average collected ($26,700) falls below break-even, meaning some months the partners are working to cover overhead before generating any personal income
  • The gap between billings and collections narrows the window between covering overhead and generating meaningful partner compensation — growing revenue without fixing collections means that gap widens at scale
Blind Spot: A structured accounts receivable system recovers revenue the firm has already earned — without a single new client, a single new ad, or a single additional hour of casework. If a formal AR workflow improved the collection rate from 78% to 90%, that would add approximately $44,000 per year in recovered revenue at current billing levels. At $600,000 in billings, the same improvement would add $72,000 per year.
The Positive
  • The firm uses Clio to track billing and collection data — knowing the 75–80% collection rate and the $30k break-even means the partners have the financial data infrastructure needed to act systematically on improvements
  • Revenue is growing year over year toward the $600k goal — the underlying business is performing; the profit issues are systems gaps, not market or talent gaps
The Gap
  • Without a formal collections process, the gap between billings and collections will persist — and at $600k in billings, a 78% collection rate would mean $132,000 in uncollected earned revenue per year
  • The break-even-to-collections dynamic means that in lower-volume months, the firm is working primarily to cover overhead rather than generating wealth for the partners — creating a cycle that makes it difficult to invest in growth
  • Without profit planning beyond the break-even number, the partners have limited visibility into what they are each actually earning per hour worked — making it impossible to make strategic decisions about which cases, clients, or service lines to prioritize
Fixing collections is the fastest path to more money in the partners' hands — it does not require a single new client, only a system to recover what the firm has already earned.
