Intake Assessment

Adler Law is generating more than 100 internet leads per month — but the consultation-to-client conversion rate has fallen from 90% to 60%, meaning that for every 10 families who sit down with the firm, 4 walk away without becoming clients. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Consultation-to-client conversion has dropped from 90% to 60% — a 30-point decline that Steven identified but has not yet been diagnosed or corrected
  • No automated follow-up system exists for prospects who attend a consultation but do not sign immediately — these leads disappear without any structured nurture sequence
  • No clearly documented intake process that Marissa or a dedicated intake team member can own independently of Steven
  • After-hours coverage for inbound leads — calls and contact form submissions arriving after 5 PM — has not been confirmed as consistently staffed or systematically handled
Blind Spot: The 30-point drop in conversion is almost certainly not about lead quality — Scorpion is still delivering over 100 per month. Something in the consultation experience or the post-consultation follow-up process has changed. Identifying and fixing that single variable could recover $6,000–$30,000 per month without spending an additional dollar on marketing.
The Positive
  • The firm is generating 100+ leads per month — the raw material for intake improvement is already there; no new lead generation investment is required to see immediate results from fixing conversion
  • A 60% close rate remains above industry average for estate planning consultations — the baseline is strong enough that targeted fixes will have outsized impact on recovered revenue
The Gap
  • At the current 40% non-conversion rate with 100+ leads per month, the firm may be leaving 1–5 cases per month unclaimed — at $6,000 average, that is $6,000–$30,000 per month in recoverable revenue that is currently walking out the door
  • Prospects who consult and leave without signing are actively comparing firms — without follow-up, they call the next firm on the list the same week
  • Without a documented intake process Marissa can run independently, Steven cannot step back from the sales role — the conversion problem stays his personal responsibility
Every case that closes without Steven being in the room is a direct step toward the firm Marissa can run — and every lost consultation that gets no follow-up is a step backward from the 2.5-day week Steven is working toward.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • Contact form location and functionality on sawlaw.com could not be verified due to site access restrictions — must be confirmed live before this presentation to assess whether forms are prominently placed and converting
  • Live chat presence on the site could not be confirmed during this research — given the firm's 100+ leads per month from Scorpion, any after-hours or real-time chat capability that is missing represents a gap in capturing inbound contacts at the moment of highest intent
  • Web-generated leads that submit a contact form outside business hours need a defined response protocol — the speed of first contact after a form submission is one of the strongest predictors of whether that lead converts to a consultation

Team Assessment

Adler Law has an eight-person team — four attorneys including Marissa and four paralegals — but the firm runs through Steven on banking, administration, and key decisions. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Steven personally handles banking and administrative tasks that should be delegated to Marissa or a firm administrator — keeping him operationally involved in work that does not require an attorney
  • The eight-person team operates without KPIs or scorecards — there is no visibility into individual performance, case progression, or team accountability, which means Steven has to stay close to the work to catch problems
  • Marissa is being groomed for leadership but lacks the systems infrastructure — defined roles, accountability framework, delegation authority — to actually run the firm independently of Steven
  • Pending hires (a paralegal and a bookkeeper through Cashroom) add headcount without adding accountability systems — creating more coordination work for Steven in the short term, not less
Blind Spot: The fact that Marissa is trusted and capable is actually the core issue in reverse — it means Steven is likely delegating case work to her without delegating the authority and systems to make firm-level decisions without him. Transition requires structure, not just trust.
The Positive
  • An eight-person team with a junior partner already in place is a genuine foundation — many firms at this revenue level are still solo or near-solo, making Adler Law structurally further along than it may feel from the inside
  • Marissa as the identified successor gives the firm a clear leadership transition path that most small firms never establish at all
The Gap
  • Without KPIs and scorecards, Steven cannot see where performance gaps exist — which means he must stay close to the day-to-day to catch problems before they cost the firm cases or clients
  • The pending hires add headcount without accountability infrastructure, creating a larger team that is harder to manage while Steven is still the management layer
  • As long as administrative tasks like banking sit with Steven personally, his 2.5-day week cannot be achieved — even if every other problem in the firm is solved
The path to working 2.5 days per week is not just about Marissa being ready — it is about building the accountability infrastructure that lets both Steven and Marissa know the firm is running correctly without Steven checking in every day.

Profit Plan Assessment

Adler Law generated $1.4 million in revenue last year and is projecting $1.8–$2 million for the current year — but without real-time financial visibility, profit is discovered at the end of the year rather than planned for throughout it. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Financials are tracked manually in Excel — a manual process that creates delays, introduces errors, and requires Steven's personal involvement to maintain rather than running automatically
  • The Clio–QuickBooks sync is broken — billing data and financial records are disconnected, creating gaps in the firm's true financial picture that the new Cashroom bookkeeper has been hired to address
  • No profit-per-case-type visibility — the firm does not know whether estate planning, probate, real estate, or guardianship cases are most profitable per hour invested, making marketing investment decisions essentially guesswork
  • Fixing the Clio–QuickBooks sync through Cashroom is a data plumbing fix, not a profit plan — the technology repair solves the measurement problem but does not replace the need for a structured financial operating system
Blind Spot: Revenue of $1.4 million with no case profitability tracking means the firm could be working hardest on the least profitable practice areas. At a $6,000 average case value across five different practice areas with very different complexity levels, the real profit per matter almost certainly varies significantly — and without tracking it, the firm cannot make intelligent decisions about which case types to take more of and which to refer out.
The Positive
  • Revenue of $1.4 million demonstrates real economic scale — the firm has the raw numbers to build a profit plan that actually works, which is more than most small firms at this stage can say
  • The Cashroom bookkeeper hire shows Steven recognizes the financial tracking problem and is taking concrete steps to address it — the foundation for better financial systems is being laid
The Gap
  • Without a real-time financial dashboard, Steven cannot know whether hitting $1.8 million in revenue will actually result in taking home more — it depends entirely on whether costs scale proportionally, and right now that visibility does not exist
  • A broken Clio–QuickBooks sync means cash position and revenue recognition may not match reality — potentially masking profitability problems until they become a cash crisis rather than a planning issue
  • Without a profit plan, Steven cannot answer the critical question: what does the firm need to generate for him to take home a meaningful salary working 2.5 days per week — and is the current revenue trajectory actually getting him there?
The 2.5-day week Steven wants is only sustainable if the firm generates enough profit to pay his salary, Marissa's salary, the team, and a meaningful return — and right now, nobody knows exactly what that number is or whether the firm is on track to reach it.
