Here Is Where Adler Law Firm Stands Today

Steven Adler has spent 17 years building a respected estate planning and probate practice in Jericho, New York — generating $1.4 million per year with a team of eight and over $10,000 per month invested in Scorpion digital marketing. The firm is producing more than 100 internet leads per month, yet a consultation-to-client conversion rate that has fallen from 90% to 60% means dozens of paid leads are walking away every month without becoming clients.

Stage 6 is the stage where Steven works 2.5 days a week, Marissa runs the day-to-day, and the firm generates consistent income without requiring his constant presence — and that is exactly where this plan takes him.

Where You Are Today

Stage 4: Small Business Manager
  • Scorpion marketing generates 100+ internet leads per month — but intake converts only 60%, down from 90%, costing an estimated 1–5 cases per month at $6,000 each
  • Steven personally handles banking and administrative tasks that have not been delegated to anyone on the team
  • A team of 8 attorneys and paralegals operates without KPIs, scorecards, or a structured accountability system
  • Firm finances are tracked manually in Excel with a broken Clio–QuickBooks sync — no real-time financial visibility exists

Where You Need To Be

Stage 6: Law Firm Owner
  • The firm runs without you
  • Your income is no longer tied to your personal hours
  • Your time is yours to spend however you choose
  • This is where true freedom and true wealth become possible
The gap between working four days a week and working two and a half is not as wide as it feels — but closing it requires systems, not just effort.

The Law Firm Growth Acceleration Model

We Help Law Firm Owners Get More Profit, More Personal Freedom, And Outstanding Results For Their Clients.

Most law firms try to grow by chasing more leads. But leads alone are not the problem — and leads alone are not the answer. Here is what actually works.

Lead Generation

Leads are to a law firm what gasoline is to a car. Without them, you go nowhere. But a steady, predictable flow of leads changes everything — you get to choose your cases instead of taking whatever comes in.

Intake

But all the leads in the world are worthless without a system to turn them into paying clients. Most firms are losing cases they already paid for simply because their follow-up is slow or inconsistent.

Self-Managing Team

Once clients are coming in, you need a team — otherwise you end up working 90-hour weeks and results suffer. A self-managing team is what gives you the freedom to step back.

Profit Plan

And once the team is in place, most owners look at their bank account and ask: where did all the money go? That is what a profit plan solves — clarity on what you earn, what you keep, and how to grow both.

All four have to work together. If one is missing, you cannot have more profit, more freedom, and outstanding results for your clients at the same time.

  • PageSpeed scores: Mobile and desktop scores could not be verified — the site returns access errors to automated tools. Must be run live at pagespeed.web.dev before this presentation. Scorpion sites vary widely; slow mobile performance is a common issue on the platform

Blind Spot: Competitors may be bidding on branded terms like "Adler Law" or "Steven Adler attorney" as Google Ads keywords — sending name-search traffic to their own sites before prospects ever reach sawlaw.com. Profit Plan Assessment

Adler Law generated $1.4 million in revenue last year and is projecting $1.8–$2 million for the current year — but without real-time financial visibility, profit is discovered at the end of the year rather than planned for throughout it. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Financials are tracked manually in Excel — a manual process that creates delays, introduces errors, and requires Steven's personal involvement to maintain rather than running automatically
  • The Clio–QuickBooks sync is broken — billing data and financial records are disconnected, creating gaps in the firm's true financial picture that the new Cashroom bookkeeper has been hired to address
  • No profit-per-case-type visibility — the firm does not know whether estate planning, probate, real estate, or guardianship cases are most profitable per hour invested, making marketing investment decisions essentially guesswork
  • Fixing the Clio–QuickBooks sync through Cashroom is a data plumbing fix, not a profit plan — the technology repair solves the measurement problem but does not replace the need for a structured financial operating system
Blind Spot: Revenue of $1.4 million with no case profitability tracking means the firm could be working hardest on the least profitable practice areas. At a $6,000 average case value across five different practice areas with very different complexity levels, the real profit per matter almost certainly varies significantly — and without tracking it, the firm cannot make intelligent decisions about which case types to take more of and which to refer out.
The Positive
  • Revenue of $1.4 million demonstrates real economic scale — the firm has the raw numbers to build a profit plan that actually works, which is more than most small firms at this stage can say
  • The Cashroom bookkeeper hire shows Steven recognizes the financial tracking problem and is taking concrete steps to address it — the foundation for better financial systems is being laid
The Gap
  • Without a real-time financial dashboard, Steven cannot know whether hitting $1.8 million in revenue will actually result in taking home more — it depends entirely on whether costs scale proportionally, and right now that visibility does not exist
  • A broken Clio–QuickBooks sync means cash position and revenue recognition may not match reality — potentially masking profitability problems until they become a cash crisis rather than a planning issue
  • Without a profit plan, Steven cannot answer the critical question: what does the firm need to generate for him to take home a meaningful salary working 2.5 days per week — and is the current revenue trajectory actually getting him there?
The 2.5-day week Steven wants is only sustainable if the firm generates enough profit to pay his salary, Marissa's salary, the team, and a meaningful return — and right now, nobody knows exactly what that number is or whether the firm is on track to reach it.
