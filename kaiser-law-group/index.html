Here Is Where The Kaiser Law Group Stands Today

Daniel Kaiser has built a respected criminal defense practice in Flagstaff over nearly three decades — handling DUI, drug crimes, and domestic violence cases, along with personal injury and workers' compensation, out of a four-person office built largely on reputation and referrals. The firm generates $600K–$1.2M annually, but revenue is unpredictable, 2024 was a loss year, and the single most critical finding is this: with a daughter joining the firm this fall and a goal of reaching $3M in three years, The Kaiser Law Group has no consistent paid marketing system — while competitors like Griffen & Stevens are running with 294 Google reviews and paid ad presence across multiple channels.

Stage 6 is the stage Dan Kaiser needs to reach to step back to 20 hours a week, mentor his daughter as a fellow attorney, and build a firm that earns whether he is in the office or not — and that is exactly where this plan is taking him.

Where You Are Today

Stage 4: Small Business Manager

  • No paid advertising running; previous LSA attempts failed due to poor targeting
  • Dan personally handles after-hours emergency calls and fields intake directly
  • Revenue swings $600K wide year to year — 2024 reportedly ended in a net loss

Where You Need To Be

Stage 6: Law Firm Owner
  • The firm runs without you
  • Your income is no longer tied to your personal hours
  • Your time is yours to spend however you choose
  • This is where true freedom and true wealth become possible
The gap between where Dan is today and the firm he is describing — where he works 20 hours a week, mentors his daughter, and watches the revenue come in consistently — is real, but it is closeable. It just requires the right systems in the right order, starting now.

The Law Firm Growth Acceleration Model

We Help Law Firm Owners Get More Profit, More Personal Freedom, And Outstanding Results For Their Clients.

Most law firms try to grow by chasing more leads. But leads alone are not the problem — and leads alone are not the answer. Here is what actually works.

Lead Generation

Leads are to a law firm what gasoline is to a car. Without them, you go nowhere. But a steady, predictable flow of leads changes everything — you get to choose your cases instead of taking whatever comes in.

Intake

But all the leads in the world are worthless without a system to turn them into paying clients. Most firms are losing cases they already paid for simply because their follow-up is slow or inconsistent.

Self-Managing Team

Once clients are coming in, you need a team — otherwise you end up working 90-hour weeks and results suffer. A self-managing team is what gives you the freedom to step back.

Profit Plan

And once the team is in place, most owners look at their bank account and ask: where did all the money go? That is what a profit plan solves — clarity on what you earn, what you keep, and how to grow both.

All four have to work together. If one is missing, you cannot have more profit, more freedom, and outstanding results for your clients at the same time.

Intake Assessment

The Kaiser Law Group's intake system is entirely Dan-dependent — he personally handles after-hours emergency calls, makes the judgment calls on case acceptance, and relies on general support staff with no defined intake protocol. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
"Prospects convert when they speak to an attorney fast."
The Problem
  • Dan personally fields after-hours and weekend emergency calls — there is no coverage system, no virtual receptionist, and no protocol when Dan is unavailable
  • No third-party client financing in use — only in-house payment plans, which Dan described as admin-heavy and financially risky; price-sensitive prospects who cannot pay upfront are walking away from cases that could be converted with financing
  • No documented intake script for staff — calls are fielded by generalist support staff with no defined qualification criteria, no urgency language, and no handoff protocol to Dan
  • No defined speed-to-lead standard — criminal defense clients call in crisis; if Dan is unavailable and there is no escalation path, those leads convert to whoever answers fastest
Blind Spot: In criminal defense, most high-value prospects (DUI arrests, drug charges) call within hours of an incident. If the firm does not have a response in the first 30–60 minutes, those prospects have already called two or three other attorneys. Speed-to-lead is not a nice-to-have in criminal defense — it is the conversion mechanism.
The Positive
  • Dan is personally responsive — his direct availability for after-hours calls has maintained relationships and won urgent cases; the gap is the absence of a system when he is not available
  • Dan identified the problem himself on the call and named the exact solutions: attorney access, speed-to-lead, intake scripting, and financing — showing strong self-awareness about what needs to change
The Gap
  • Every after-hours call that goes unanswered when Dan is unavailable is a potential $5,000 case going to a competitor — at a 15% close rate, even 10 missed monthly contacts represents a $7,500 monthly revenue gap
  • Without third-party financing, qualified prospects who cannot pay the full retainer upfront are leaving the consultation without signing — each one is a $5,000 case that could have converted with a CallPay or Affirm option
  • Without a documented intake script, staff cannot qualify, triage, or escalate calls consistently — and when Dan eventually delegates intake to his daughter or a new hire, there is no system to hand off
Fixing intake is the single fastest way to increase revenue without increasing ad spend — and it creates the documented process Dan needs to delegate intake when his daughter joins, freeing him from the calls that are currently keeping him tethered to the phone on nights and weekends.

Profit Plan Assessment

The Kaiser Law Group's revenue has historically ranged from $600K to $1.2M in a single year — a $600,000 variance that reflects a firm where profit is discovered at the end of the year rather than planned at the beginning. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: RED
"Revenue historically swings about $600K to $1.2M gross. 2024 reportedly lost money despite paying a large tax extension check."
The Problem
  • Revenue swings $600K wide year to year — a firm with this much variance has no reliable basis for making hiring decisions, committing to new expenses, or planning for growth
  • 2024 was a net loss year — a firm billing in the normal range should not be losing money; without visibility into which costs exceeded revenue, the same outcome can repeat
  • Heavy reliance on contract criminal work creates a revenue floor that masks actual private client performance — Dan cannot clearly see which part of his revenue is scalable and which is dependent on court contracts that may not always be available
  • No third-party financing in use — in-house payment plans that carry default risk mean some portion of billed revenue is never actually collected, distorting the real picture of profitability
Blind Spot: The gap between a $600K year and a $1.2M year is almost certainly not the number of cases — it is likely a combination of case mix (more complex, higher-fee private clients vs. lower-fee contract work), collection rates, and untracked expenses. Until those drivers are identified and measured, Dan cannot reliably replicate a good year or prevent a bad one.
The Gap
  • Without a profit plan, Dan cannot confidently commit to his daughter's salary in September — a $25K/month revenue increase target is a reasonable goal, but only if there is a system tracking whether that increase is actually hitting the bottom line
  • Growing to $3M in 3 years while adding attorneys and staff requires knowing the cost structure at every stage — without financial visibility, revenue growth can easily be absorbed by costs without increasing what Dan takes home
  • A firm that does not know its numbers cannot be sold, cannot attract a partner, and cannot be stepped back from — financial clarity is the foundation of every exit and every ownership transition
The 20-hour work week and the legacy firm Dan described are both built on one foundation: knowing exactly what the firm earns, what it costs, and what it keeps. Building that visibility now — while growing revenue — is what turns a busy law practice into a real business that Dan can step back from on his own terms.
