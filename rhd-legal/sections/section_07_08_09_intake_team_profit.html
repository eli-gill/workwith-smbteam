Intake Assessment

RHD Legal has one of the most disciplined intake operations of any firm at this revenue level — 24/7 coverage, a structured multi-touch follow-up sequence, and a personal outreach process that converts referred leads at close to 95%. The challenge now is making sure the system can scale to 60 signed cases per month without Richard personally carrying it. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: GREEN
"I've got my intake dialed in."
The Problem
  • Overall conversion rate is approximately 20% across all inbound channels — the benchmark for well-managed PI PPC and LSA traffic is 55–60%, indicating meaningful conversion improvement is still available, particularly on paid leads
  • The final follow-up touch — a personal email sent directly from Richard — is the most effective step in the sequence and the one that cannot be delegated as the firm scales; it works precisely because it is Richard, and that creates a ceiling on how many leads can move through this step without his direct involvement
  • As case volume doubles toward 60 signed cases per month, the current intake structure — distributed across the team with Richard as the backstop — will need a dedicated intake owner whose sole job is conversion metrics and follow-up, not just call answering
Blind Spot: The large gap between the 95% referral conversion rate and the 20% overall conversion rate is not just a lead quality difference — it reflects the trust premium that referrals carry before they ever call. Every Meta video ad impression, every Google ad, every 5-star Google review is a system that moves cold inbound leads closer to referral-level trust. Intake is strong. The lead nurturing that happens before the call is the gap — and it is where the next conversion rate improvement lives.
The Positive
  • 24/7 answering service with after-hours call transfer and an attorney on call — inbound leads are captured at any hour, a standard most comparably-sized PI firms do not have in place
  • Four-touch follow-up sequence (email, text, email, personal email from Richard) with CallRail tracking — the process is documented, disciplined, and already producing near-95% conversion on the warmest leads
The Gap
  • At 130–160 calls per month and 20% overall conversion, approximately 26–32 cases are being signed; improving overall conversion rate from 20% to 30% on existing volume — without any new ad spend — would add 13–16 cases per month from leads already being generated
  • A dedicated intake specialist empowered to own conversion metrics end-to-end — not just answer calls — would free Richard from the final personal follow-up touch and make the system fully scalable without him
  • Doubling to 60 signed cases per month at the current conversion rate requires 300+ inbound calls per month; the intake staffing and process need to be built ahead of that volume, not in reaction to it
An intake system that runs and converts without Richard personally sending the final email is not just an operational upgrade — it is the first concrete step toward the CEO role he described in his three-year vision.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • Contact form is embedded above the fold on the homepage — this is a genuine intake conversion strength that many competitor sites do not implement correctly, and it ensures that visitors who are ready to act can engage immediately without scrolling
  • No live chat is installed — for PI website visitors who arrive after hours, are comparison shopping across multiple firms, or prefer text to form submission, the absence of an instant engagement option is a conversion leak that compounds across every paid and organic traffic source
  • Practice area and geo-specific landing pages exist across the site but are not currently tied to dedicated paid campaigns — running PPC and LSA traffic to the homepage instead of matched landing pages reduces conversion rate and increases cost per signed case

These findings are also referenced in the Website Audit section.

Team Assessment

Richard brought six loyal, experienced people with him from his previous firm — several have worked alongside him for five to ten years — and they are executing case work at a high level. The problem is not the team's capability. It is that every strategic decision, all marketing management, and the firm's business development still run through Richard personally, and there is no management layer between him and the team. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
"I'm doing it all, right? ...I was here last night till midnight doing AI videos."
The Problem
  • No dedicated marketing or business development role exists — Richard personally manages PPC strategy, LSA direction, SEO oversight, AI video content production, and social media in addition to handling cases, managing staff, and overseeing office expansion
  • The team is structured for case execution — case managers, paralegals, legal assistants — but no one's job is to track marketing performance, manage ad campaigns, or produce content without Richard's direction
  • Richard described spending "way too much" of his day on $10-per-hour tasks; without a clear delegation framework and an org chart that gives team members decision authority in their domains, this pattern continues regardless of how much revenue grows
  • The plan to add a workers' comp attorney and an overseas attorney is the right strategic thinking — but hiring additional people into a firm where Richard is still the operational hub creates more complexity, not less, without a management layer already in place to absorb them
Blind Spot: Richard is comfortable doing it all because he is genuinely excellent at it — the firm exists and is growing rapidly precisely because of his personal capability. But that same capability is what will prevent the firm from ever running without him. The most capable owners are consistently the last to delegate because they do it better than anyone else on the team. At Stage 4, that is a strength. At Stage 6, it is the ceiling.
The Positive
  • The team Richard has built is genuinely exceptional — people who have worked with him for five to ten years, who know the vision and the standard, and who execute case work without being micromanaged. That foundation is rare and valuable.
  • Richard has a specific, articulated three-year vision — particular offices, particular states, a defined role for himself — that gives the firm a roadmap most owners never have. That clarity is a real operational advantage when building toward it.
The Gap
  • Without a marketing or operations person empowered to own growth systems, every new case Richard signs creates more work for Richard personally — there is no leverage, and the ceiling on the firm's growth is the ceiling on his available hours
  • Adding the workers' comp attorney, the Spanish-language practice, and the Southern Pines expansion will increase operational complexity faster than a six-person team can absorb without a dedicated operations or leadership role to manage it
  • The version of RHD Legal Richard described — multiple attorneys, multiple states, Richard attending two court appearances a year by choice — requires a management layer between him and the casework team that does not currently exist and will not appear without being intentionally built
The multi-state firm Richard described in year three — multiple attorneys, offices across Virginia, South Carolina, and Georgia, him in the courtroom twice a year because he wants to be — only becomes possible when there is someone empowered to run the firm between Richard and the casework team.

Profit Plan Assessment

RHD Legal has grown from zero to nearly $1 million in under two years — a trajectory that most law firms never achieve in their first decade. The challenge now is converting that revenue growth into a predictable profit engine that Richard can measure, plan against, and scale with confidence. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
"This year we'll do over a million."
The Problem
  • Case acquisition cost is tracked at the click level — Richard confirmed approximately $500 per click — but not at the signed-case level by channel. Without knowing the true cost per signed case from PPC, LSA, referrals, and organic separately, every marketing budget decision is based on intuition rather than data.
  • The 60/40 referral-to-marketing split is cost-efficient today, but the flip Richard wants — 40% referrals, 60% marketing — will significantly increase acquisition costs. Without a profit plan built around that shift, growing marketing spend could increase revenue while compressing margins.
  • Richard described spending too much time on $10-per-hour tasks — but that time has a financial cost that does not appear in any report. The hours spent managing ad campaigns, producing video content, and handling operational details represent lost revenue that a profit plan would make visible and motivating to fix.
  • PI revenue is contingency-based, which means income lags signed cases by 12–24 months — planning cash flow, reinvestment, and team compensation against projected settlements rather than actual receipts requires financial modeling that most firms at this stage have not yet built
Blind Spot: The multi-state expansion Richard has mapped out — Virginia, South Carolina, Georgia — will require understanding not just whether cases can be won in those states, but whether cases in those markets produce better economics than NC cases. North Carolina's contributory negligence rules can cap case values in ways that neighboring states' laws do not. A profit plan that includes practice area economics and state-by-state case value analysis is not a luxury for a firm at this stage — it is the foundation for making smart expansion decisions rather than expensive ones.
The Positive
  • Revenue has more than doubled from year one to year two — the underlying business model, lead flow, and case quality are working
  • Richard already tracks key marketing metrics (cost per click, LSA spend, inbound call volume via CallRail) — the data foundation exists. It just needs to be organized into a profit plan that translates channel activity into case economics.
The Gap
  • Without knowing cost per signed case by channel, Richard cannot make confident decisions about where to scale ad spend and where to cut — and the firm may be over-investing in channels with poor case economics while under-investing in channels that produce the best returns
  • As the firm passes $1 million and approaches $2 million, financial decisions grow more complex — compensation structure, tax planning, reinvestment versus distribution, case reserves for contingency settlements — and making those decisions reactively instead of proactively is how fast-growing firms develop cash flow problems despite strong revenue
  • The multi-state expansion plan requires knowing exactly what it costs to open and run a market before committing — profit planning turns that decision from a guess into a calculation
The freedom Richard described — stepping back from casework, funding multi-state expansion, making strategic decisions from a CEO chair — only becomes real when he has a clear picture of what the firm earns, what it keeps, and what it costs to grow.
