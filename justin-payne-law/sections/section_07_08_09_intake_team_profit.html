Intake Assessment

At The Law Offices of Justin R. Payne, PA, every intake interaction currently depends entirely on the attorney — phone calls during business hours, personally managed, with no online form and no coverage outside a five-day, eight-and-a-half-hour window. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Attorney personally handles all intake — every new inquiry requires Justin to stop billable work to answer or return a call, with no defined process for routing or qualifying prospects
  • No contact form exists on the homepage — the only path to contact is a phone number, and prospects who arrive outside business hours have no option but to wait or call someone else
  • Office hours are Monday–Friday 8:30am–5pm — the firm is unreachable evenings, weekends, and holidays, which are precisely when legal emergencies and urgent decisions tend to surface
  • No defined follow-up sequence for inquiries that do not immediately convert — a prospect who calls, does not reach anyone, and does not hear back within hours is likely retained by a competitor by the following morning
Blind Spot: Most people searching for an attorney outside of a personal referral do so in the evening or on a weekend — when a contract dispute has just escalated, a property transaction has just collapsed, or a business partner has just sent a demand letter. The firm's current intake infrastructure turns those high-urgency moments into voicemail, and many prospects will not wait until Monday morning.
The Gap
  • Every unanswered after-hours inquiry represents a qualified prospect who is likely retained by a competitor before the next business day — at $350/hour and multi-hearing civil litigation case values, a single lost case per month represents $5,000–$25,000 or more in foregone revenue
  • No intake script means conversion depends entirely on the attorney's availability and energy in the moment — inconsistent, unscalable, and personally taxing
  • No follow-up cadence means prospects who express interest but do not immediately retain have no structured reason to return — they fall out of the funnel with no recovery mechanism
Building an intake system that converts without Justin's direct involvement is the first step toward the supervisory practice he described — and toward a firm that generates revenue whether or not he is the one who picked up the phone.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • No contact form exists on the homepage — the primary conversion page has no mechanism for capturing a prospect's information outside of a direct phone call
  • The "Contact Us" navigation link leads to a separate page that returns only a phone number and address — no form, no scheduling option, and no digital path to engagement
  • No after-hours intake mechanism is in place — the website goes dark as a lead capture tool every evening, weekend, and holiday

These findings are also referenced in the Website Audit section.

Team Assessment

Justin R. Payne runs a solo practice with one recently hired assistant — a structure where every function from intake to legal work to administration flows through a single person. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED
"What would success look like in 12 months? More automation; supervisory role; win more cases — emulate 'wizard' model."
The Problem
  • Justin is the sole attorney — every case requires his personal involvement at every stage from first call through trial, with no associate or paralegal to delegate file preparation, discovery management, or client communications
  • The recently hired assistant has no defined role, documented responsibilities, or accountability system — the cost of the hire is being absorbed without fully recapturing Justin's time
  • 10–20 hours per week are spent on non-legal administrative tasks that have not been successfully delegated — consuming the highest-value hours of a senior litigator
  • The practice pivot decision (probate vs. criminal defense) has been delayed, which in turn delays the hiring decision that either path requires — paralegal for probate, associate for criminal — leaving the firm structurally frozen
Blind Spot: The 10–20 hours per week Justin spends on non-legal tasks is the cost equivalent of a full-time administrator — but instead of delegating that work to someone earning $40,000–$50,000 per year, it is consuming the time of a $350/hour litigator. At conservative estimates, that represents $182,000–$364,000 per year in opportunity cost. Solving that single problem — defining the assistant role fully and hiring the right next person — has a higher ROI than almost any marketing investment.
The Positive
  • A first team member has been hired — the initial step toward delegation exists, and a foundation is in place to build on with the right role definition and accountability structure
The Gap
  • Without a second attorney or qualified paralegal, Justin cannot take time off, handle a personal matter, or be sick without the firm stalling — there is no backup for any function
  • The administrative hours consuming 10–20 hours per week are preventing Justin from doing the high-value legal work that generates revenue — he is capped by his own available time, and more marketing will only increase that pressure
  • A solo practice with no management structure has limited market value — buyers of law firms purchase documented systems, trained teams, and recurring revenue, not a single attorney's personal reputation
The wizard practice Justin described — staff prepares the files, he argues the cases, and the firm runs on defined systems — is a team design problem, not a talent problem. Building that team starts with role definition, and role definition starts now.

Profit Plan Assessment

At approximately $350,000 in annual revenue, Justin R. Payne has built a genuinely profitable solo practice — but without visibility into margins, cost per case, or a financial plan, growth is an aspiration rather than a strategy. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Revenue is known (~$350,000/year) but profit margin is not — a $350/hour billing rate does not reveal what the firm actually keeps after overhead, bar fees, insurance, and operational costs
  • No financial tracking by practice area — without cost visibility, it is impossible to evaluate whether real estate litigation, property insurance disputes, or commercial matters produce the best margins per case
  • The practice pivot decision (probate vs. criminal defense) is a capital allocation choice being made without the financial data to evaluate which path is more profitable — that is a significant strategic risk
  • No acquisition cost tracking — without knowing what each retained case costs to attract and convert, there is no basis for evaluating the return on any marketing investment
Blind Spot: The 10–20 hours per week spent on non-legal admin is not accounted for in any profit analysis. If Justin is billing 30 hours per week but spending 15 additional hours on tasks that generate no revenue, his effective hourly return drops well below $350. A profit plan would quantify this drag precisely — and justify the investment in staff that eliminates it.
The Positive
  • Revenue of ~$350,000 as a solo civil litigator is a genuine indicator of market demand and professional reputation — this is a strong financial base that justifies and rewards investment in a growth system
The Gap
  • Without knowing profit by case type, the practice pivot decision is being made on preference rather than numbers — choosing the wrong pivot direction could compress margins at exactly the moment Justin is trying to grow
  • A firm growing in revenue without a profit plan often finds that margins compress as costs scale — more cases, more staff, and more complexity can mean less personal income without financial visibility to catch the drift
  • The exit Justin described requires documented financials — buyers evaluate net profit and transferable systems, not gross revenue, and a firm without financial records commands a fraction of what a well-documented practice is worth at sale
The firm Justin wants to eventually sell — the one with transferable systems, documented profitability, and a track record a buyer can underwrite — cannot be built without first understanding what it currently earns, what it keeps, and where the gaps are.
