Intake Assessment

The intake process at the Law Office of Jason A. Guida is the most urgent revenue problem in the entire firm — not because of a lack of leads, but because the system to capture and convert those leads is broken at multiple points simultaneously. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED — Critical Priority
"The 'check-in after agreement sent' queue is neglected, leaving an estimated $50k+ in potential revenue on the table."
The Problem
  • 52+ leads have been sitting in MyCase since January with only a single voicemail and no further follow-up — an estimated $50,000+ in potential revenue is currently unworked
  • The intake assistant does not follow the established script — key data including the 90-day statute of limitations for license denials is consistently missed on calls, which can directly harm clients' legal options
  • The "How did you hear about us?" field in MyCase is blank on nearly every record — the firm has no data on which lead sources are working, making any marketing investment a guess
  • Post-agreement check-in queue is systematically neglected — signed clients are not being followed up after the engagement letter goes out, creating both revenue leakage and a professional risk
  • Only one voicemail is left per lead with no text, no email, and no structured multi-touch follow-up sequence — in today's market, a single outreach attempt recovers a fraction of available leads
Blind Spot: The 52 leads in MyCase since January are not lost — they are paused. Many of those individuals still have a legal problem that has not been resolved. A structured reactivation campaign run through those leads today could recover a significant portion of that $50,000+ estimate with zero additional marketing spend.
The Gap
  • At a conservative average case value of $3,500, the 52 unworked leads represent approximately $182,000 in potential revenue at a 100% conversion rate — even converting 30% of them would return $54,600 to the firm from leads already in the pipeline
  • Without tracking lead sources in MyCase, Jason cannot identify which referral relationships or organic searches drive the highest-value cases — every future marketing investment is made without a baseline
  • The missed statute of limitations data creates legal risk: if a license denial client misses their 90-day window because intake failed to flag it, the consequences extend far beyond lost revenue
Fixing intake is not about marketing — it is about keeping the money Jason has already earned. A firm that converts leads reliably is a firm that funds itself, that funds the attorney hire, and that eventually funds Jason stepping back.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • The contact form is on a separate page (/home/contact/) rather than embedded above the fold on the homepage — prospects who land on the homepage and do not scroll may leave without finding any way to reach the firm
  • No live chat or instant response mechanism was confirmed on the site — after-hours visitors have no friction-free path to submit a message and receive any acknowledgment
  • There is no confirmed appointment-booking widget on the website — prospects who visit outside business hours and cannot call must remember to call back, and many will not

Team Assessment

The Law Office of Jason A. Guida runs almost entirely on Jason — he is the attorney handling cases, the business developer managing referral relationships, and the supervisor correcting intake errors. There is no structure in place that would allow him to step back, take a week off, or even accept more cases without personally doing more hours. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED — Critical Priority
"Jason is at capacity, forcing him to decline high-value cases (e.g., USCCA) due to court conflicts."
The Problem
  • Jason personally handles all court appearances — when scheduling conflicts arise, high-value referral cases from USCCA are declined, costing the firm thousands in lost revenue per instance
  • The one team member (the assistant) is not performing to standard despite feedback: intake scripts are ignored, key data fields are left blank, and follow-up queues are neglected — the team is adding workload rather than reducing it
  • No KPIs, scorecards, or accountability framework exists — there is no objective measure of whether the assistant is improving, declining, or meeting any standard
  • No org chart, no defined roles beyond "attorney" and "assistant," and no plan for who does what when a second attorney is hired — the structure that would support growth does not yet exist
Blind Spot: The plan to hire an attorney to cover court appearances is the right first move — but hiring without a defined role, accountability system, or onboarding process often results in a new person who creates new problems rather than solving the old ones. The structure needs to be built before the hire, not after.
The Positive
  • Jason has already identified the right hire: an attorney to cover court appearances, which would directly free him to focus on business development and high-value case intake
  • The firm uses MyCase as its case management platform — a foundation that can support structured intake workflows, task assignment, and team accountability once properly configured
The Gap
  • Every USCCA case declined due to a court conflict is direct revenue lost — and there is no way to calculate how many of these are happening annually without financial tracking
  • Without accountability structures, the assistant's performance issues will persist regardless of how many times they are verbally corrected — the behavior requires a system, not just a conversation
  • Without an org structure in place before the attorney hire, Jason risks bringing on a second person into the same broken system — and the problems multiply rather than shrink
The firm Jason described — one he can step back from, work in part-time, and eventually sell — requires people who perform without him watching. Building that starts with defining roles, establishing accountability, and making the first hire in the right order.

Profit Plan Assessment

The Law Office of Jason A. Guida is generating meaningful revenue — approximately $400,000 annually — but has no visibility into where that money is going, how much is being kept, or what the firm can afford to invest in growth. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER — In Progress
"No formal bookkeeping or financial projections; Jason manages finances reactively."
The Problem
  • No formal bookkeeping system or financial projections exist — Jason manages cash flow reactively, making decisions without data on margins, expenses, or profitability by practice area
  • No tracking of cost per lead, cost per acquisition, or marketing ROI — the "How did you hear about us?" field being blank in MyCase means the firm cannot measure which lead sources generate the best cases
  • The attorney hire Jason plans — which is the key to unlocking growth — is a significant financial decision being made without a financial model; the wrong hire at the wrong revenue level can compress margins severely
  • An estimated $50,000+ in revenue is leaking annually through unworked intake follow-up — this is a financial problem as much as it is an intake problem, and it is invisible without tracking
Blind Spot: Jason described wanting a bookkeeper who can provide strategic advice on hiring and growth — not just someone who categorizes expenses. That distinction matters. A strategically oriented financial advisor can tell him exactly when the revenue threshold is right to hire the attorney, what that hire does to his take-home pay, and how long before the investment pays back. Without that, the hire is a bet rather than a decision.
The Positive
  • Jason has a clear revenue goal ($500,000) and has identified the key lever to get there (hiring an attorney to handle court appearances and free himself for higher-value work) — the strategic thinking is sound, it just lacks the financial infrastructure to execute safely
The Gap
  • Without financial visibility, Jason cannot determine whether the current $400k estimate is accurate, whether margins are healthy enough to support a new hire, or whether specific practice areas are carrying the firm while others drain it
  • The absence of cost-per-acquisition data means any marketing investment Jason makes — even with SMB Team — cannot be measured against a baseline, making it impossible to optimize or report true ROI
  • A firm built to be eventually sold requires documented, predictable profitability — investors and buyers evaluate firms on financial history, not potential; that history needs to start being built now
The sellable firm Jason described is not valued on revenue — it is valued on documented, predictable profit. Building that starts with knowing the numbers today.
